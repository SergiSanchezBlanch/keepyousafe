Trick or treat

It was a popular legend of Celtic origin according to which not only the spirits of the deceased were free to roam the Earth on Halloween night, but all kinds of entities from all the spiritual realms. Among them was a terribly malevolent one who wandered through towns and villages, going from house to house asking for trick or treat. The legend says that the best thing was to make a deal, no matter what the cost, because if he did not agree with this spirit, he would use his powers to do trickery, which would be to curse the house and its inhabitants, giving them all kinds of misfortunes and curses, how to make the family sick, kill cattle with pests or even burn their own homes.

Jack-o'-lantern

In the seventeenth century, the Irish and Scottish celebrated Halloween by lighting small lamps at the entrances to their homes to chase away evil spirits and lost souls like Stingy Jack. In rural areas they carved turnips, beets or potatoes with sinister faces and put a small candle inside.
